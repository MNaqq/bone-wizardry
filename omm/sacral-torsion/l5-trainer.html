The L5 Question

You got the torsion right. But do you actually understand what L5 is doing?

Scene I — The Attending's Office
The fluorescent lights buzz overhead. The kind of lights that make everyone look a little more dead inside than they already are. A third-year medical student stands in the doorway, clutching a patient chart like a life raft.
You are the attending. You have coffee. You have patience. You will need both.
Student: "I... I diagnosed the patient. Left-on-left sacral torsion." (They say this like they've just defused a bomb. Their hands are shaking.)
You: "Okay. Walk me through how you got there."
Student: "The patient's physical exam showed..."
Patient Physical Findings
  • Positive seated flexion test on the LEFT
  • Deep sacral sulcus on the LEFT
  • Posterior/inferior ILA on the RIGHT
  • Lumbosacral spring test: sacrum springs freely (negative)
How the student read it
1
Deep sulcus on LEFT → sacral base moved anterior on LEFT → rotation = LEFT
2
Seated flexion positive on LEFT → SI joint restricted on left = left oblique axis engaged → axis = LEFT
3
Rotation LEFT on LEFT oblique axis → named: Left on Left  (same sides = forward torsion)
✓
Spring test negative (springs freely) → confirms forward torsion
Student: "Left-on-left forward torsion." (They look at you with the energy of a golden retriever who just brought you a stick.)
You: "That's correct. Nice work."
A flicker of joy crosses the student's face. It will not last.
You: "So because of their sacrum, their back hurts?"
Student: "...yes?" (The joy has left.)
You: "Right. But is it just the sacrum that's affected? Is it just those areas that are the problem?"
Student: "...yes?" (Their voice goes up at the end. They know this is wrong. They don't know why.)
You set down your coffee. This is the part they never teach well enough. The sacrum doesn't rotate in isolation. Something else moves with it. Something at L5.
What do you ask the student next?
Scene II — The Real Question
You: "In a left-on-left sacral torsion, what is L5 doing?"
Student: "..." (The student stares at you like you've asked them to calculate pi to the 50th digit.)
This is the question. The one that separates knowing the diagnosis from understanding it. The sacrum rotates left. L5 sits right on top of it. It doesn't just... sit there.

Scene III — But Why?
You: "L5 rotates to the RIGHT. Good. But why?"
Student: "Because... torsion?" (Said with the confidence of someone guessing their own birthday.)
You lean forward. This is where it clicks — or where it doesn't.

Scene IV — The Full Picture
You: "So now you don't just have a sacral torsion. You have a sacral torsion AND an L5 somatic dysfunction. What does that open up?"
Student: "...more treatment options?" (A spark of understanding. The golden retriever is learning a new trick.)
You: "Exactly. You can treat the sacral torsion directly, or you can address L5. Sometimes you need both. And on the boards — they will absolutely test whether you know what L5 is doing."
The student writes something down. Then crosses it out. Then writes it again. Progress.
The Rule: In a sacral torsion, L5 couples with the sacrum. The direction depends on whether it's forward or backward.
Torsion Type Fryette's Law L5 Rotation
L on L Forward Type I (opposite) Right
R on R Forward Type I (opposite) Left
L on R Backward Type II (same) Left
R on L Backward Type II (same) Right
Why it matters: Knowing L5's position (1) confirms your torsion diagnosis, (2) gives you additional treatment targets, and (3) is a favorite board question.
The student looks up from their notes. Something has shifted behind their eyes. Not confidence — not yet. But the beginning of it. The part where the pieces start fitting together instead of floating around in the dark.
Student: "So if someone asks me 'what is L5 doing in an R-on-L torsion,' it's rotated right? Because backward = Type II = same side?"
You: "Now you're getting it."
You take a sip of coffee. It's cold now. Worth it.
