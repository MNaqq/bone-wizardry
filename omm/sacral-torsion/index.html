Sacral Torsion Diagnosis

Axes, landmarks, flexion tests, and the anterior/posterior confusion — all broken down step by step.

1
The Two Oblique Axes
▼

The sacrum rotates around two oblique axes that run diagonally across it:

↗ Left Oblique Axis — runs from the upper left to the lower right of the sacrum. Named for where it starts (superior pole = left).
↖ Right Oblique Axis — runs from the upper right to the lower left of the sacrum. Named for where it starts (superior pole = right).

Think of it like this: the axis is named for the superior (top) attachment side. Left oblique = left SI joint on top.

💡 The sacrum engages the opposite axis during gait. When you walk and your trunk rotates left, the sacrum rotates on the right oblique axis. This is normal physiologic motion.
2
Naming a Torsion: "X on X"
▼

A sacral torsion is named by two things:

1. The direction of rotation — which way the sacral base rotated (Left or Right)
2. The axis it rotated around — Left oblique or Right oblique

Format: Rotation ON Axis

"Left on Left" = Rotated LEFT on LEFT oblique axis

The four sacral torsions:

TorsionRotationAxisType
L on LLeftLeft obliqueForward (physiologic)
R on RRightRight obliqueForward (physiologic)
L on RLeftRight obliqueBackward (non-physiologic)
R on LRightLeft obliqueBackward (non-physiologic)
💡 Same = Forward. Different = Backward. If the rotation and axis are on the SAME side (L on L, R on R) = forward/physiologic torsion. If they're on DIFFERENT sides (L on R, R on L) = backward/non-physiologic.

Why "forward" vs "backward"?

In a forward torsion, the sacral base on the rotation side moves anteriorlyAnterior = toward the front of the body. When you feel a DEEP sacral sulcus from behind, the base moved FORWARD (anterior) into the body. Deep from behind = anterior movement. (into the body). This happens during normal gait — it's physiologic.

In a backward torsion, the sacral base on the rotation side moves posteriorlyPosterior = toward the back. When you feel a SHALLOW sacral sulcus, the base moved backward toward your fingers. Shallow = posterior = closer to the surface.. This is non-physiologic and usually from trauma or strain.

3
Landmark Findings — The 2x2 Grid
▼

You palpate four landmarks from behind the patient:

☝ Sacral Sulcus (L + R) — the groove between the sacrum and the ilium, near the base. You're feeling for deep vs shallow.
☝ ILA — Inferior Lateral Angle (L + R) — the bottom corners of the sacrum. You're feeling for posterior (prominent) vs anterior (less prominent).

The critical translation (this trips everyone up):

⚠ Deep sulcus = base went ANTERIOR (forward, into the body). You feel it as deep because the bone moved AWAY from your fingers. Even though you're touching the patient's BACK, "deep" means the structure went toward the FRONT.
☝ Shallow sulcus = base went POSTERIOR (backward, toward you). You feel it as shallow/full because the bone came TOWARD your fingers.

Master grid for all four torsions:

TorsionL SulcusR SulcusL ILAR ILAType
L on L Deep Shallow Anterior (less prominent) Posterior (prominent) Forward
R on R Shallow Deep Posterior (prominent) Anterior (less prominent) Forward
L on R Shallow Deep Posterior (prominent) Anterior (less prominent) Backward
R on L Deep Shallow Anterior (less prominent) Posterior (prominent) Backward
💡 Pattern: The deep sulcus and the posterior ILA are always on opposite sides. Deep sulcus = base went forward on that side; posterior ILA = inferior pole kicked backward on the OPPOSITE side. They're the same rotation viewed from two ends of the sacrum.
4
Seated vs Standing Flexion Test
▼

These tests tell you WHERE the dysfunction is — sacral vs iliac:

🪌 Seated Flexion Test — patient sits (takes the ilia/pelvis out of the equation). If positive on one side, the problem is at the SACRUM (sacroiliac joint). The PSIS that rises more = the restricted side.
🧍 Standing Flexion Test — patient stands (includes iliac motion). If positive on one side, the problem could be iliac OR sacral. Need seated test to differentiate.

For sacral torsions specifically:

🔎 A positive SEATED flexion test confirms sacral involvement. The side that's positive = the side of the axis (the oblique axis engages that SI joint). For R on R torsion, the seated flexion test is positive on the RIGHT.
TorsionPositive Seated FlexionAxis
L on LLeftLeft oblique
R on RRightRight oblique
L on RRightRight oblique
R on LLeftLeft oblique
💡 The seated flexion test tells you the AXIS, not the rotation direction. The positive side = the axis name. You need the sulcus/ILA findings to determine which direction the sacrum rotated.
5
L5 Coupling — The Confirmation Rule
▼

In a sacral torsion, L5 rotates opposite to the sacrum. This is a coupling rule you can use to confirm your diagnosis.

🔄 Forward torsion (physiologic): L5 rotates to the OPPOSITE side of the sacral rotation. R on R torsion = L5 rotated LEFT. L on L torsion = L5 rotated RIGHT.
🔄 Backward torsion (non-physiologic): L5 rotates to the SAME side as the sacral rotation. R on L torsion = L5 rotated RIGHT. L on R torsion = L5 rotated LEFT.
TorsionL5 RotationRule
L on LRightOpposite (forward)
R on RLeftOpposite (forward)
L on RLeftSame (backward)
R on LRightSame (backward)

Why does this happen?

In a forward torsion, the sacrum is moving the way it should during gait. L5 counter-rotates to maintain balance — Type I mechanicsFryette's Type I: sidebending and rotation occur to OPPOSITE sides. This happens in neutral (group curve). Type I governs the compensatory motion of L5 in forward sacral torsions.. In a backward torsion, the sacrum and L5 are stuck moving together — Type II mechanicsFryette's Type II: sidebending and rotation occur to the SAME side. This happens in flexion or extension (single segment). Type II governs backward torsions where L5 and sacrum are locked together..

Forward = Fryette Type I (opposite)   |   Backward = Fryette Type II (same)
6
The Anterior/Posterior Trap
▼

This is the #1 source of confusion for every student learning sacral mechanics. Let's kill it.

⚠ The problem: You're palpating from the BACK (posterior). A deep sulcus means the bone went FORWARD (anterior). But "forward" and "deep" feel like opposites when you're touching from behind.

The mental model:

Imagine you're behind the patient, fingers on the sacral sulcus. Now imagine the sacral base as a seesaw:

YOUR FINGERS (posterior)   ←  SACRAL BASE  →  PATIENT'S BELLY (anterior)
Base tips AWAY from you
= DEEP sulcus
= ANTERIOR sacral base
Base tips TOWARD you
= SHALLOW sulcus
= POSTERIOR sacral base
💡 Rule: Always think P → A. When diagnosing, think about where the sacral base WENT. Deep = it went anterior. Shallow = it stayed posterior. The direction the base moved tells you the rotation.

For the ILA, it's the reverse end of the seesaw:

☝ Posterior ILA = prominent, you can feel it sticking out. The inferior pole kicked BACKWARD (toward you). Anterior ILA = tucked in, less palpable. The inferior pole went FORWARD (away from you).

If the base went anterior on one side (deep sulcus), the ILA on that SAME side went anterior too (less prominent). The OPPOSITE ILA kicks posterior (more prominent). It's all one rotation.

COMLEX Speed Run — Sacral Torsions
▼

On COMLEX, sacral torsion questions give you landmarks and a flexion test. Here's the fastest way to the answer:

1. Find the deep sulcus. That tells you the rotation direction (base went anterior on that side = sacrum rotated toward that side).
2. Check the seated flexion test. The positive side = the axis name.
3. Put it together: [Rotation side] on [Axis side]. Deep sulcus on R + positive seated flexion on R = R on R.
4. Forward or backward? Same sides = forward. Different sides = backward.

The full reference table:

R on R Right Left Right Springs L rot Forward
L on R Right Left Right Rigid L rot Backward

⚠ Board trap: L on R and R on R LOOK alike on landmarks (same deep sulcus, same posterior ILA). The spring test differentiates them. Forward = springs. Backward = rigid. If you only get sulcus + ILA + seated flexion, you have TWO possible answers — you NEED the spring test (or L5 finding) to pick one.

Practice Vignette — Walk Through It
▼
📋 A 35-year-old patient presents with low back pain. On exam, you find a deep right sacral sulcus, a posterior left ILA, and a positive seated flexion test on the right. The lumbosacral spring test shows the sacrum springs freely. L5 is rotated left. What is the sacral diagnosis?

Think through it:

Deep right sulcus = sacral base went anterior on the right = rotation is to the RIGHT.

Positive seated flexion on the right = axis is on the RIGHT = RIGHT oblique axis.

Rotation RIGHT on RIGHT oblique = R on R.

Same sides = Forward torsion (confirmed by spring test: springs freely).

L5 rotated left = opposite to sacral rotation (consistent with forward/Type I).

A. Left on Left sacral torsion
L on L would have a deep LEFT sulcus and positive seated flexion on the LEFT. The landmarks are on the wrong side for this answer.
B. Right on Right sacral torsion ✓
Deep R sulcus + posterior L ILA + positive R seated flex + springs freely + L5 rotated L = R on R forward torsion. All findings are consistent.
C. Left on Right sacral torsion
L on R has the same deep R sulcus and positive R seated flex, but it's a BACKWARD torsion. The sacrum would be rigid (not spring freely), and L5 would rotate to the SAME side (left = same as sacral rotation left). Here, the sacral rotation is RIGHT, not left.
D. Right on Left sacral torsion
R on L would have a deep LEFT sulcus and positive seated flexion on the LEFT. Wrong side for all landmarks.
⚠
Board Traps — Don't Fall For These
▼
⚠ Trap 1: Thinking "deep sulcus = posterior." WRONG. Deep = anterior. The bone moved AWAY from your fingers (into the body). This is the most common mistake.
⚠ Trap 2: Confusing the seated flexion test with the standing flexion test. Seated = sacral dysfunction. Standing = could be iliac. For sacral torsions, you need the SEATED test.
⚠ Trap 3: Mixing up "forward" and "backward." Remember: same sides = forward, different sides = backward. L on L and R on R = forward. L on R and R on L = backward.
⚠ Trap 4: Forgetting the spring test differentiates forward from backward when landmarks are ambiguous. R on R and L on R share the same deep sulcus side and seated flex side. Only the spring test (and L5) separates them.
⚠ Trap 5: Thinking Type I/II refers to the TORSION type. No — Type I/II are Fryette's laws describing L5 coupling. Forward torsions follow Type I (opposite rotation). Backward torsions follow Type II (same rotation).
