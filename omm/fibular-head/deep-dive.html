1
The Big Picture: Why Does the Fibula Matter?
▼

The fibula connects the knee to the ankle. It's involved in the biomechanics of both joints. Pain over the lateral proximal leg = think fibular head dysfunction.

🦿 The fibula moves like a SEESAW — when the proximal end goes anterior, the distal end goes posterior, and vice versa.

This seesaw motion means: any dysfunction at one end creates a reciprocal dysfunction at the other end. You can't have an anterior fibular head without a posterior lateral malleolus.

2
Seesaw Biomechanics: Supination vs Pronation
▼

Everything comes down to what the foot does:

Foot MotionComponentsDistal FibulaTalusProximal Fibula
Supination Adduction + Inversion + Plantarflexion Glides anterior Internally rotates Glides posterior
Pronation Abduction + Eversion + Dorsiflexion Glides posterior Externally rotates Glides anterior
💡 “SIP” = Supination = Inversion + Plantarflexion (+ Adduction)
Proximal goes POSTERIOR
⚡ Board shortcut: Supination = proximal posterior. Pronation = proximal anterior. Opposite ends, opposite directions. Always.
3
Exam Findings: Anterior vs Posterior
▼

The dysfunction is named for where the fibular head IS, not where it resists motion. But the exam tests what motion is restricted.

FindingAnterior Fibular HeadPosterior Fibular Head
Proximal fibula resists... Posterior glide Anterior glide
Ease of motion (proximal) Anterior glide (it's already there) Posterior glide (it's already there)
Lateral malleolus resists... Anterior glide Posterior glide
Talus position Externally rotated Internally rotated
Foot is... Pronated (ABD + eversion + DF) Supinated (ADD + inversion + PF)
Foot resists... Supination Pronation
🎯 Board trap: "Resists posterior translation" = the head is stuck anterior. The dysfunction is named for WHERE IT IS, the exam finding is what direction it WON'T GO.
4
Muscle Energy Treatment (Post-Isometric)
▼

Post-isometric muscle energy is a direct technique. You position the patient into the restrictive barrier, then the patient pushes toward ease while you resist (isometric).

🔑 THE KEY: Position into the barrier = position the foot in the direction it RESISTS. For anterior fibular head, the foot resists supination, so position INTO supination (adducted, inverted, plantarflexed).

Anterior Fibular Head — Muscle Energy Steps:

  1. Position foot in plantarflexion, inversion, adduction (supination) to engage the restrictive barrier
  2. Monitor the proximal fibular head with your thumb and index finger
  3. Patient pushes foot into dorsiflexion, eversion, abduction (toward ease/pronation) for 3–5 seconds
  4. Physician provides counterforce (isometric) and imparts a posterior force to the fibular head
  5. Engage the new restrictive barrier, repeat 3–5 times
  6. Reassess fibular head motion
💡 Remember: Direct = into barrier. Patient pushes toward ease. Physician resists. 3–5 sec × 3–5 reps.

Posterior Fibular Head — Muscle Energy Steps:

  1. Position foot in dorsiflexion, eversion, abduction (pronation) to engage the restrictive barrier
  2. Monitor the proximal fibular head
  3. Patient pushes into plantarflexion, inversion, adduction (toward ease/supination) for 3–5 seconds
  4. Physician provides counterforce and imparts an anterior force to the fibular head
  5. Engage new barrier, repeat 3–5 times
  6. Reassess
5
HVLA Treatment
▼

HVLA is also a direct technique — position into the barrier, then apply a quick thrust.

VariableAnterior Fibular HeadPosterior Fibular Head
Patient position Supine, knee extended Supine, knee & hip flexed 90°
Foot position Supinated (PF + INV + ADD), leg internally rotated Pronated (DF + EV + ABD), leg externally rotated
Physician contact Thenar eminence on anterior fibular head MCP of index finger on posterior fibular head
Thrust direction Anterior → Posterior (downward) Posterior → Anterior (with knee flexion)
⚡ Board trap: For anterior HVLA the knee is extended. For posterior HVLA the knee is flexed to 90°. Don't mix these up!
6
Board Traps & Speed-Run Cheat Sheet
▼
🚨 Trap #1: "Resists posterior glide" ≠ posterior dysfunction. It means the head is stuck anteriorly and can't go posterior. The dysfunction is ANTERIOR.
🚨 Trap #2: For muscle energy, you position into the barrier (supination for anterior), but the patient pushes toward ease (pronation). Don't confuse who moves which direction.
🚨 Trap #3: Answer choices will mix & match components. "Adducted, inverted, and dorsiflexed" is WRONG because dorsiflexion belongs to pronation, not supination. All three must match: ADD + INV + PF = supination.
🚨 Trap #4: Post-isometric (patient pushes toward ease) vs isotonic/isolytic (different activating forces). COMLEX loves specifying "post-isometric" to test if you know the positioning still goes INTO the barrier.
💡 SPEED RUN:

Anterior fibular head = foot pronated at rest = supinate to treat
Posterior fibular head = foot supinated at rest = pronate to treat

Muscle energy: position INTO barrier, patient pushes TOWARD ease
HVLA: position INTO barrier, thrust THROUGH barrier

Supination = ADD + INV + PF (SIP!)
Pronation = ABD + EV + DF

1. A patient's right proximal fibular head resists posterior translation. What is the somatic dysfunction?

✅ Correct! "Resists posterior translation" means the fibular head is stuck anteriorly. The dysfunction is named for WHERE IT IS — anterior.
❌ The dysfunction is named for where the fibular head IS, not where it resists motion. "Resists posterior glide" = stuck anterior = anterior fibular head.

2. To treat an anterior fibular head with post-isometric muscle energy, the foot is initially positioned in:

✅ Correct! Muscle energy is direct — position INTO the restrictive barrier. Anterior fibular head resists supination, so supinate the foot: ADD + INV + PF.
❌ Muscle energy is direct: position INTO the barrier. Anterior fibular head = foot resists supination (ADD + INV + PF). That's the initial positioning. Watch for mixed components!

3. During supination of the foot, the proximal fibular head glides:

✅ Correct! Seesaw rule: supination pulls the distal fibula anterior, so the proximal end goes posterior. SIP = Supination, proximal goes Posterior.
❌ Remember the seesaw: supination = distal fibula goes anterior = proximal fibula goes POSTERIOR. Opposite ends, opposite directions.

4. For HVLA treatment of an anterior fibular head, the patient's knee should be:

✅ Correct! Anterior fibular head HVLA = knee extended, foot supinated, thenar eminence on anterior fibular head, thrust A→P.
❌ For anterior fibular head HVLA: knee EXTENDED. For posterior: knee flexed to 90°. Don't mix them up!

5. A patient with a posterior fibular head somatic dysfunction has a foot that is at rest in:

✅ Correct! Posterior fibular head = foot is supinated at rest (ease of motion is in supination). The foot resists pronation, which is the restrictive barrier.
❌ Posterior fibular head: the foot is in supination at rest (that's its ease of motion). It resists pronation. Seesaw: supinated foot = proximal fibula posterior.

6. In post-isometric muscle energy for anterior fibular head, after positioning into the barrier, the patient is asked to push their foot toward:

✅ Correct! Post-isometric ME: physician positions INTO barrier (supination), patient pushes TOWARD ease (pronation = DF + EV + ABD), physician resists isometrically.
❌ In post-isometric ME, the patient always pushes TOWARD EASE while the physician resists. Ease for anterior fibular head = pronation (DF + EV + ABD).

7. A patient has an anterior fibular head. What is the position of the talus?

✅ Correct! Anterior fibular head = foot is pronated = talus is externally rotated. Posterior = talus internally rotated.
❌ Anterior fibular head = pronated foot = talus EXTERNALLY rotated. Posterior = supinated foot = talus internally rotated.
