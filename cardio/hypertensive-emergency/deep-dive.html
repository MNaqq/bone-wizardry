1
The Big Picture: Emergency vs Urgency
▼

First, know the difference. Boards LOVE this distinction:

Hypertensive EmergencyBP > 180/120 WITH acute end-organ damage. This is what kills people — you must treat NOW with IV meds. = BP > 180/120 + organ damage
Hypertensive UrgencyBP > 180/120 WITHOUT organ damage. Scary numbers but the patient is stable. Treat with oral meds, bring down slowly over 24-48 hours. = BP > 180/120 + no organ damage

The organs at risk — and how damage presents:

OrganHow damage shows up
BrainEncephalopathy, stroke, headache, vision changes, papilledema
HeartAcute MI, aortic dissection, acute heart failure / pulmonary edema
KidneysAcute kidney injury, hematuria, rising creatinine
EyesRetinal hemorrhages, papilledema
VesselsAortic dissection, microangiopathic hemolytic anemia
⚠ Board rule: Emergency = IV drugs in the ICU. Urgency = oral drugs, bring down slowly. Dropping BP too fast in urgency can cause stroke.

Goal: Reduce MAP by no more than 25% in the first hour. Then gradually to 160/100 over the next 2-6 hours. Exception: aortic dissection (drop FAST).

2
Fenoldopam — The Kidney Protector
▼
Fenoldopam is a(n) D1 receptor agonist that may be used in the treatment of hypertensive emergency to prevent kidney damage

How does it work?

FenoldopamA selective dopamine D1 receptor agonist. Given IV only. Onset in 5 minutes, duration 30-60 minutes. Easy to titrate. selectively activates D1 receptors on renal and splanchnic vasculatureThe blood vessels supplying the kidneys and gut. D1 receptors here cause vasodilation, increasing blood flow to these organs even as systemic BP drops.. This does two things at once:

1. Drops BP — Peripheral vasodilation reduces systemic vascular resistance. Afterload goes down, BP goes down.
2. Protects the kidneys — D1 activation dilates renal arteries, increasing renal blood flow and GFR. It also promotes natriuresisExcretion of sodium in urine. By blocking Na⁺ reabsorption in the proximal tubule, fenoldopam acts as a mild diuretic without depleting potassium as aggressively as loop diuretics. (sodium excretion). So even while BP drops, the kidneys get MORE blood, not less.

This is why fenoldopam is the go-to when you're worried about renal damage during a hypertensive crisis. Other IV drugs drop BP but may decrease renal perfusion. Fenoldopam does the opposite.

FeatureFenoldopam
ReceptorD1 agonist (selective)
RouteIV infusion only
Onset5 minutes
Duration30-60 minutes
Renal effectIncreases renal blood flow
Special useHTN emergency with renal impairment
Side effectsTachycardia (reflex), headache, flushing
⚠ Contraindication: Glaucoma. Fenoldopam increases intraocular pressure. This is a classic board trap — don't pick fenoldopam if the patient has glaucoma.
💡 Board pearl: "Hypertensive emergency + renal failure" = think Fenoldopam. It's the ONLY antihypertensive that increases renal perfusion while lowering BP.
3
The Full Drug Lineup
▼

Boards want you to match the right drug to the right scenario. Here's the lineup:

💊 Fenoldopam
D1 agonist — renal vasodilation + systemic vasodilation

USE: HTN emergency with renal compromise. Protects kidneys while dropping BP.

Renal protective Avoid in glaucoma
💊 Nitroprusside
NO donorReleases nitric oxide (NO), which activates guanylate cyclase → cGMP → smooth muscle relaxation. Dilates both arteries AND veins — drops preload AND afterload. — arterial + venous dilation

USE: Most HTN emergencies (first-line historically). Watch for cyanide toxicity with prolonged use (>48-72h). Monitor thiocyanate levels.

Cyanide risk Light-sensitive
💊 Labetalol
Alpha-1 + Beta blockerBlocks both alpha-1 (vasodilation) and beta-1/beta-2 (slows heart, reduces contractility). The alpha:beta ratio is 1:3 IV. Does NOT cause reflex tachycardia. — combined blockade

USE: Pregnancy (eclampsia), stroke, aortic dissection. Safe in pregnancy — that's the big differentiator.

Safe in pregnancy No reflex tachy
💊 Nicardipine
DHP Calcium Channel BlockerDihydropyridine CCB — selectively blocks L-type calcium channels in vascular smooth muscle. Vasodilates without significantly affecting heart rate or conduction (unlike verapamil/diltiazem). — arterial vasodilation

USE: Stroke, post-op hypertension. Predictable, easy to titrate. Good cerebral vasodilation.

Stroke-friendly Easy titration
💊 Clevidipine
Ultra-short-acting DHP CCB — arterial only

USE: Peri-operative HTN. Metabolized by esterases in bloodBroken down by enzymes in the blood itself — doesn't need liver or kidney. So it works the same regardless of organ function. Half-life is ~1 minute. (not liver/kidney). Half-life ~1 min.

Ultra-short Lipid emulsion — avoid in egg/soy allergy
💊 Esmolol
Beta-1 selective blockerOnly blocks beta-1 receptors (heart). Ultra-short acting — metabolized by red blood cell esterases. Half-life ~9 minutes. Perfect when you need precise, brief beta-blockade. — ultra-short acting

USE: Aortic dissection (with nitroprusside). Rate control priority. Also peri-op.

Aortic dissection Ultra-short
💡 Aortic dissection rule: ALWAYS start beta-blocker FIRST (esmolol or labetalol) to drop heart rate, THEN add vasodilator if needed. If you vasodilate first, reflex tachycardia increases aortic shear stress and worsens the dissection.

5
Practice Vignette — Walk Through It
▼
📋 A 58-year-old man with a history of CKD stage 3 and diabetes presents to the ED with BP 220/130, headache, and blurry vision. Labs show creatinine 3.2 mg/dL (baseline 1.8). Urinalysis shows proteinuria and hematuria. What is the best IV antihypertensive?

Think through it:

BP > 180/120 + headache + blurry vision + rising creatinine = hypertensive emergency with acute kidney injury.

The creatinine jumped from 1.8 to 3.2 — this patient's kidneys are taking a hit RIGHT NOW. You need a drug that drops BP while protecting renal blood flow.

A. Nitroprusside
Powerful but can cause cyanide toxicity and may decrease renal perfusion. Not ideal when kidneys are already failing — they can't clear thiocyanate well either.
B. Labetalol
Good general choice, but it doesn't offer specific renal protection. Fine for many emergencies, but not the BEST when the stem screams "save the kidneys."
C. Fenoldopam ✓
D1 agonist that lowers BP while increasing renal blood flow. CKD + rising creatinine + hypertensive emergency = fenoldopam is the answer. It's the only choice that actively protects the kidneys.
D. Esmolol
Ultra-short beta-blocker. Great for aortic dissection and rate control, but doesn't offer renal protection. Not the best choice when AKI is the main concern.
6
Board Traps — Don't Fall For These
▼
⚠ Trap 1: Picking fenoldopam for a patient with glaucoma. Fenoldopam increases intraocular pressure. If the stem mentions glaucoma, pick a different drug.
⚠ Trap 2: Starting a vasodilator first in aortic dissection. ALWAYS beta-blocker first. Vasodilators alone cause reflex tachycardia which increases aortic wall shear stress.
⚠ Trap 3: Confusing emergency vs urgency. High BP alone (even 220/130) is NOT an emergency without organ damage. Don't slam IV drugs for urgency — you can cause a stroke from dropping BP too fast.
⚠ Trap 4: Using ACE inhibitors in acute hypertensive emergency. ACE-i are ORAL and slow-acting — they're for long-term management, not acute crises. Boards may tempt you with enalaprilat (IV ACE-i) but it's unpredictable and risky in renal artery stenosis.
⚠ Trap 5: Forgetting that nitroprusside needs thiocyanate monitoring and is dangerous in renal/hepatic failure. Cyanide can't be cleared properly.
